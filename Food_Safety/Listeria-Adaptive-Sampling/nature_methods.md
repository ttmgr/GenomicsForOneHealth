# Methods

## Quasi-Metagenomic Sample Preparation and Sequencing
To enhance the relative abundance of *Listeria monocytogenes* prior to sequencing, environmental and food-associated samples underwent a brief, non-selective primary enrichment step outlining a quasi-metagenomic approach. Enriched samples were subjected to genomic DNA extraction using standard protocols optimized for Gram-positive microbial communities. Libraries were subsequently prepared for Oxford Nanopore Technologies (ONT) sequencing according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Sequencing was performed utilizing MinION flow cells. To facilitate direct benchmarking, separate sequencing runs were performed for each sample evaluating two paradigms: Native (N) sequencing representing the unenriched microbiome background, and Adaptive Sampling (AS) aimed at the real-time enrichment of *Listeria* targets.

## Adaptive Sampling and Basecalling
During AS runs, the sequencing instrument utilized real-time selective sequencing software to map incipient DNA translocations against a predefined *Listeria* reference assembly. Reads lacking homology to the target reference were computationally rejected and physically ejected from the nanopore by voltage reversal, accelerating the acquisition of target-specific molecules. Following sequencing runs, raw signal data (POD5 format) for both N and AS replicates were converted to sequence reads using Dorado (Oxford Nanopore Technologies), utilizing high-accuracy or super-accurate (SUP) deep-learning models. To assure contiguous downstream assembly, subsequent read demultiplexing and rigorous adapter trimming were performed using Porechop. High-quality sequences were retained following length filtering (≥100 bp threshold) executed via NanoFilt.

## Taxonomic Profiling and Target Extraction
To rigorously assess the microbial background complexity and calculate the relative enrichment efficiency afforded by AS, all quality-filtered reads were taxonomically classified using Kraken2 against a comprehensive custom genomic database. Following background community profiling, taxonomic classifications were parsed, and reads confidently assigned to the *Listeria* genus (or specifically *L. monocytogenes*) were selectively extracted from the composite FASTQ files employing seqtk. This extraction step isolated the target population for subsequent high-resolution structural analysis.

## De Novo Assembly and Consensus Polishing
To evaluate the impact of AS enrichment on genome contiguity and plasmid recovery, the extracted target reads for each sample replicate underwent independent *de novo* assembly utilizing three distinct long-read algorithms structured to manage complex metagenomic variance: metaMDBG, Flye (operated in `--meta` mode), and Myloasm. Draft assemblies produced by these respective tools were subsequently polished to mitigate persistent indel sequencing errors. Raw reads were aligned back to the primary draft assemblies utilizing the `dorado aligner` module; the resulting alignments were processed through the `dorado polish` module incorporating the `--bacteria` optimized neural network model to generate the final, high-fidelity genome and plasmid consensus sequences.

## Assembly Annotation and Comparative Benchmarking
Functional interrogation of the finalized assemblies was performed to deduce the presence of critical virulence markers and antimicrobial resistance (AMR) determinants. AMRFinderPlus was employed in its extended mode (`--plus`), mapping both complete contigs and raw sequences against its comprehensive reference databases to identify actionable public health phenotypes. To quantify the holistic utility of the comparative methods, bespoke analytical scripts integrated overall sequence yields against read-length dropout rates (evaluating the physics underpinning AS molecule ejection). Concurrently, final assembly quality—assessed via metrics such as contiguity (N50), target enrichment percentage, and genome fraction recovery—was systematically compared across the matched AS and Native sequencing cohorts.
