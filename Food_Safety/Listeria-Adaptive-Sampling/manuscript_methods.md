# Bioinformatics Methods

## Basecalling and Quality Control
Basecalling of raw multiplexed Oxford Nanopore sequence data initially stored in POD5 and FAST5 formats was executed using Dorado (v0.7.0, Oxford Nanopore Technologies), utilizing high-accuracy or super-accurate (SUP) deep-learning models (`dna_r10.4.1_e8.2_400bps_sup`). The resultant sequence alignments and unmapped sequence records in BAM format were extracted into compressed FASTQ streams utilizing SAMtools (v1.19). To guarantee structurally contiguous sequences prior to assembly, demultiplexing and exhaustive adapter trimming were performed using Porechop (v0.2.4). High-quality contiguous sequences were retained following length filtering (discarding reads <100 bp) executed via NanoFilt (v2.8.0), and quality metrics were consolidated via NanoStat (v1.6.0). 

## Taxonomic Profiling and Target Extraction
To rigorously assess the microbial background complexity and accurately quantify the relative enrichment efficiency afforded by Adaptive Sampling (AS), all quality-filtered reads were taxonomically classified utilizing Kraken2 (v2.1.3) against a centralized, custom genomic database. Following background community profiling, resultant taxonomic classifications were parsed. Reads confidently assigned (at the Lowest Common Ancestor level) to the *Listeria* genus, or specifically *Listeria monocytogenes*, were selectively extracted from the composite matched FASTQ files employing Seqtk (v1.4) referencing the filtered identification headers. This step successfully isolated the target genomic sub-populations for subsequent high-resolution structural assembly.

## De Novo Assembly and Consensus Polishing
To critically evaluate the impact of AS enrichment on genome contiguity, structural accuracy, and plasmid recovery, the target-specific read fractions for each sample replicate underwent independent *de novo* assembly. Three distinct long-read algorithms structured to manage complex, uneven metagenomic variance were employed identically across replicates: metaMDBG (v0.3.0), Flye (v2.9.3; operated strictly in `--meta` mode), and Myloasm (v0.1.0). To mitigate persistent insertion and deletion biases characteristic of nanopore single-molecule reads, draft assemblies produced by these respective tools were subsequently polished. Extracted target reads were aligned back to the primary draft assemblies utilizing the `dorado aligner` module (yielding coordinate-sorted BAM files via Minimap2); the resulting alignments were systematically processed through the `dorado polish` module incorporating its structurally optimized `--bacteria` neural network configuration.

## Assembly Annotation and Comparative Benchmarking
Functional interrogation of the finalized assemblies was performed to deduce the presence of critical virulence markers and antimicrobial resistance (AMR) determinants. AMRFinderPlus (v3.12.8) was employed in its extended mode (`--plus`), mapping both assembled contigs and raw quality-filtered sequences against its heavily curated reference profile Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) to identify actionable public health phenotypes. 

To quantify the holistic utility of the comparative methods, bespoke analytical scripts integrated overall sequence yields against read-length distributions (evaluating length dropout signatures indicative of the physics underpinning AS molecule ejection). Concurrently, final assembly quality—assessed via standard contiguity metrics (N50), target enrichment ratios, and ultimate genome/plasmid target recovery fractions—was systematically compared across the matched AS and Native sequencing cohorts.
